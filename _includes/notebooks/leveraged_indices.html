In this post we want to examine leveraged indices. The idea of leveraged indices is to increase the exposure to the a certain index. Hence, a leveraged index should come with higher risks but also higher return potential than the original underlying index itself.

First, we show how leveraged indices can be constructed manually. The idea behind leveraged indices is quite simple: one just needs to duplicate the original investment into a certain index. Hence, one does not simply by the underlying index once, but through borrowing of additional money one buys into a certain market multiple times. For a leverage factor of 2 and an initial investment of 100 this means: borrow additional 100 and invest the total amount of 200 into the index. Borrowing money will come at the expense of costs in the form of interest rate payments.

We will now manually compute the evolution of a daily leveraged index on the EURO STOXX 50, where borrowing can be done at EONIA rates. The following charts show normalized prices for the EURO STOXX 50 Index and EONIA rates in percentages. All data is from Bloomberg.

When manually computing leveraged index returns we will apply the following index methodology:

Leveraged Daily Index Methodology

Let $R$ denote the total return of the underlying index between two dates t-1 and t, and $r_f$ denote the annual overnight risk-free rate. Furthermore, let $g$ denote the leverage ratio (or leverage factor), and $T$ denote the number of calendar days between dates t-1 and t. Then the leveraged daily index total return $R_L$ is defined by

$$R_L=g \cdot R +(1-g) \cdot r_f \cdot \frac{T}{360}$$

(e.g. see the leveraged index methodology of MSCI)

For the sake of simplification we will allow the following two imprecisions:

  • in reality, $T$ can be 1 day during business weeks, 3 days for Friday to Monday returns, and something else depending on holidays. On average, however, with roughly 250 index returns per year, the risk-free rate will be held for $\frac{1}{250}$ days
  • if $R$ denotes the index return from t-1 to t, then we need to subtract the risk-free rate prevailing in t-1

Daily returns of the leveraged index hence can be computed as follows:

Instead of strictly ensuring the desired leverage factor on a daily basis, one could also restore the leverage ratio on lower frequencies only. For example, monthly leverage indices only ensure the desired leverage ratio at the beginning of each month, such that the leverage ratios in between will vary subject to market moves. In this case, one can not borrow at EONIA overnight rates anymore, because the money will be needed for a full month now.

Now that we already have a basic understanding of how leverage indices are constructed, let's further examine the impacts of leveraging. Using a leverage factor of 2 we roughly get 2 times the daily return of the underlying index each day. But what does that mean in the long run? Should we expect to get twice the return also there?

To answer that question, let's first compare the performances of the EURO STOXX 50 Index with the daily and monthly leveraged indices. In addition, we also show a synthetic time series that for each day is computed as twice the performance of the EURO STOXX 50 Index. This time series shall represent the target multiple by showing the values that one might be inclined to expect by naively applying the leverage factor of 2 to longer time periods.

As we can see, while the EURO STOXX 50 Index did have a slightly positive performance over the given time period, both leveraged indices result in a strong negative performance. The long-term performance of both leveraged indices hence significantly deviate from the performance that one might expect by naively applying the leverage factor to longer time horizons. So how can that be?

First of all, the chart already indicates a crucial flaw in the naive multiplication logic: the synthetic time series takes on values below zero in 2003. Obviously this is not possible, as prices can not drop below zero (if they really reached zero at some point, then this would be a final state as the time series could never recover again).

The differences between the true leveraged index performances and the synthetic and naive computation is caused by compounding effects and is sometimes referred to as volatility drag. In the long run, leveraged indices tend to deviate from the targeted multiple significantly. In terms of risk, however, both leveraged indices seem to keep their promise: annualized volatility (using simple square root of time scaling on daily returns) is roughly two times the level as for the underlying index, and maximum drawdowns are significantly worse as well:

Let's now analyse how this volatility drag distorts the targeted performance and whether it systematically works against the investor. Since we already know how to manually compute leveraged index values given the trajectory of the underlying index, we can easily analyse the effects of the volatility drag in a simulation study. For the sake of simplicity, we will assume constant borrowing costs equal to the average EONIA rate, as we already have seen that this should not have a major impact on results. Furthermore, we use the following settings for the simulation study:

  • regular index performances following a Brownian motion: for logarithmic returns we assume an annual compounding of 6%, while the standard deviation is chosen to match the volatility of logarithmic returns of the EURO STOXX 50 index
  • simulation of 2000 paths, each of length 5000 (corresponding to roughly 20 years with 250 business days per year)
  • a leverage factor equal to 2

# specify costs of borrowing
avg_eonia = eonia.mean().squeeze()

# set parameters of normal distribution of logarithmic returns
log_mu = 0.06 / 250
log_rets = np.log(data['SX5T Index'].pct_change() + 1)
log_sigma = log_rets.std()

# specify leverage factor and scale of simulation study
n_reps = 2000
n_obs = 5000
lev_fact = 2

# preallocate output
all_normal_perfs = np.empty([n_obs, n_reps], dtype=float)
all_lev_perfs = np.empty([n_obs, n_reps], dtype=float)

for ii in range(0, n_reps):
    # draw random innovations
    log_innovs = np.random.normal(loc=log_mu, scale=log_sigma, size=n_obs)

    # compute regular index performance
    disc_gross_rets = np.exp(log_innovs)
    sim_perfs = np.cumprod(disc_gross_rets)

    # compute leveraged index performance
    disc_net_rets = disc_gross_rets - 1
    disc_rets_lev = disc_net_rets * lev_fact - (lev_fact-1) * avg_eonia/100 * 1/250
    sim_lev_perfs = np.cumprod(disc_rets_lev + 1)
    
    # store simulated paths
    all_lev_perfs[:, ii] = sim_lev_perfs
    all_normal_perfs[:, ii] = sim_perfs
